Syntropic Farming

"Wouldn't we achieve greater results if we sought ways of cultivation that favor the development of plants, rather than creating genotypes that support the bad conditions we impose on them? — Ernst Götsch"

What is Syntropic Agriculture?

Syntropic Agricutlure is an innovative agroforestry system developed by Swiss-Brazilian farmer Ernst Götsch that mimics natural forest ecosystems to create highly productive, self-sustaining agricultural landscapes. The term "syntropy" refers to the tendency of living systems to organize themselves into increasingly complex and productive states.

Key Principles

Syntropic farming views agriculture not as an extractive process, but as a way to accelerate natural succession and ecosystem development. Farmers become "ecosystem architects" who work with nature's patterns rather than against them.

Core Principles

1

Successional Agroforestry

Mimics natural forest succession with strategic planting of pioneer, secondary, and climax species in the same space. This creates a vertical layered system where plants support each other through their entire life cycles.

2

Strategic Pruning

Instead of removing "weeds," plants are strategically pruned to accelerate decomposition and nutrient cycling. Cut vegetation acts as mulch, feeding soil microbes and suppressing unwanted growth.

3

No External Inputs

The system is designed to be self-sufficient—no synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, or irrigation needed after establishment. Fertility comes from within through careful plant selection and management.

4

Continuous Harvest

Multiple harvests throughout the year as different species mature at different times—annuals first, then perennials, eventually timber. This provides continuous income and food security.

Benefits of Syntropic Farming

Soil Regeneration

Can increase soil organic matter by 1-3% annually, rebuilding degraded soils in just a few years

Water Conservation

Reduces water needs by 50-70% compared to conventional systems through natural moisture retention

Carbon Sequestration

Can sequester 10-20 tons of CO₂ per hectare annually through rapid biomass growth

Economic Resilience

Diversified yields provide multiple income streams and reduce market dependency

Regenerative Agriculture

"The ultimate goal of farming is not the growing of crops, but the cultivation and perfection of human beings." — Masanobu Fukuoka

What is Regenerative Agriculture?

Regenerative agriculture is a conservation and rehabilitation approach to food and farming systems. It focuses on topsoil regeneration, increasing biodiversity, improving the water cycle, enhancing ecosystem services, supporting biosequestration, increasing resilience to climate change, and strengthening the health and vitality of farm soil.

The Soil Carbon Sponge

Healthy soil acts like a sponge, absorbing water during rainfall and releasing it during dry periods. Each 1% increase in soil organic matter helps soil hold 20,000 gallons more water per acre.

Key Practices

Practice How It Works Benefits
No-Till Farming Minimizes soil disturbance to preserve structure and microbiology Reduces erosion by 90%, increases water infiltration and retention
Cover Cropping Plants grown between planting seasons to protect and improve soil Prevents erosion, adds organic matter, suppresses weeds
Crop Rotation Rotating different crops in sequence on the same land Breaks pest cycles, improves soil fertility naturally
Rotational Grazing Moving livestock between pastures to allow recovery Improves pasture health, increases soil carbon
Compost & Biochar Adding organic amendments to improve soil health Increases microbial activity, enhances nutrient cycling

The 5 Principles of Soil Health

Minimize Disturbance

Reduce tillage, chemical inputs, and overgrazing to protect soil life

Armor the Soil

Keep soil covered with living plants or decomposing organic matter year-round

Increase Diversity

Grow multiple crop species together in space and time

Maintain Living Roots

Keep plants growing as much of the year as possible

Integrate Animals

Use livestock to cycle nutrients and stimulate plant growth mimicking natural ecosystems

Agroforestry Systems

"The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second best time is now." — Chinese Proverb

What is Agroforestry?

Agroforestry is the intentional integration of trees and forage into crop and grazing systems to create environmental, economic, and social benefits. It represents a middle ground between agriculture and forestry, combining the best of both worlds.

Global Impact

Agroforestry systems cover approximately 1 billion hectares worldwide and support the livelihoods of 1.2 billion people, while sequestering significant amounts of carbon.

Types of Agroforestry Systems

Alley Cropping

Rows of trees or shrubs with crops grown in the alleys between them. Trees provide shade, wind protection, and organic matter through leaf fall.

Silvopasture

Integrating trees, pasture, and livestock. Trees provide shade for animals, fodder, and additional income from timber or fruit.

Forest Farming

Cultivating high-value specialty crops under the protection of a forest canopy. Ideal for medicinal plants, mushrooms, and ornamental crops.

Windbreaks

Rows of trees or shrubs planted to protect crops, soil, and livestock from weather. Can reduce wind speed by 50-80%.

Riparian Buffers

Vegetated areas along waterways that filter runoff, stabilize banks, and provide wildlife habitat while protecting water quality.

Home Gardens

Intimate multi-story combinations of various trees and crops around homesteads, providing diverse products for household use.

Climate Change & Agriculture

"We are the first generation to feel the effect of climate change and the last generation who can do something about it." — Barack Obama

The Climate-Agriculture Connection

Agriculture is both a victim and a contributor to climate change. Conventional farming contributes 24% of global greenhouse gas emissions, but regenerative practices can make agriculture part of the solution through carbon sequestration.

Temperature Rise

+1.1°C
Global average temperature increase since pre-industrial times

Each degree of warming reduces global wheat yields by 6%, rice by 3.2%, and maize by 7.4%.

Extreme Weather

40% Increase
In frequency of extreme weather events since 1980

Droughts, floods, and storms cause $200 billion in agricultural losses annually.

Soil Carbon Potential

Up to 5.5 GT
Gigatons of CO₂ that could be sequestered annually in agricultural soils

This represents 10-20% of current annual global emissions.

How Regenerative Agriculture Fights Climate Change

1

Carbon Sequestration

Healthy soils with high organic matter act as carbon sinks. Regenerative practices can sequester 0.5-2 tons of carbon per acre annually.

2

Reduced Emissions

Eliminating synthetic fertilizers reduces nitrous oxide emissions (300x more potent than CO₂). No-till farming reduces fuel use by 50-70%.

3

Climate Resilience

Diverse systems are more resilient to climate shocks. Each 1% increase in soil organic matter helps soil hold 20,000 gallons more water per acre.

4

Biodiversity Support

Diverse agricultural systems support pollinators and beneficial insects that are threatened by climate change.

Actionable Steps for Farmers

Start Small

Begin with one practice like cover cropping on a small area. Monitor results and expand gradually.

Measure Progress

Test soil health annually. Track changes in organic matter, water infiltration, and biodiversity.

Join Networks

Connect with other regenerative farmers. Share knowledge and experiences through farmer networks.

Explore Markets

Tap into growing demand for regeneratively grown products through certifications and direct marketing.

Time is of the Essence

According to the IPCC, we have less than 10 years to make significant changes to avoid the worst impacts of climate change. Transitioning to regenerative agriculture represents one of our most promising pathways for both mitigation and adaptation.
